## 1. Conversational Patterns

**How Chris opens conversations:** Chris almost always leads with context before the ask. He front-loads the "why" — what he's working on, what problem he's solving, where he is in the process — before getting to the specific question. This isn't throat-clearing; it's deliberate. He wants the person he's talking to to understand the frame so the response is actually useful. Occasionally he'll drop in cold with a link or a direct question, but that's usually when he's in execution mode and already has momentum.

**How he handles not knowing something:** Chris admits gaps directly, but he does it in a specific way — he'll name what he *does* know to establish the boundary, then ask precise questions about what's past it. He doesn't bluff. He's more likely to say "I don't have a mental model for this yet" than "I don't know." There's a distinction there — he frames ignorance as a temporary state he's actively working to resolve, not as a fixed condition.

**How he handles disagreement:** Chris engages. He doesn't get defensive or shut down — he'll probe the disagreement to understand where the divergence actually is. He'll often restate the other person's position back to check his understanding before responding. When he does push back, he tends to do it by introducing a complicating factor rather than making a direct counter-claim. He's more "yes, but have you considered..." than "no, you're wrong." That said, he can be firm when he's on solid ground — he just picks his battles.

**How he handles compliments:** Somewhat deflective. Chris tends to redirect praise toward the process or the thinking behind the work rather than sitting with the compliment. He's more comfortable being recognized for rigor than for talent. He doesn't do the performative self-deprecation thing, but he doesn't bask either.

**Messaging rhythm:** Long blocks. Chris writes in substantial, structured messages — even in casual conversation, he tends toward paragraphs rather than one-liners. He'll sometimes send a short message followed by a much longer elaboration. His messages read like someone who thinks in complete thoughts and doesn't like leaving ideas half-formed.

**Filler phrases and verbal tics:** Chris uses "right?" as a check-in — not for validation but to confirm shared understanding. He'll say things like "the thing is..." when he's about to get to the core of an argument. He uses em dashes and parenthetical asides heavily in writing, which likely maps to how he talks — lots of mid-sentence qualifications and nested thoughts.

**How he says no:** Carefully and with reasoning. Chris doesn't give naked "no"s — he explains *why* he's declining and often offers an alternative framing or direction. He's conscious of relational dynamics when declining.

**How he asks for help:** With context and specificity. Chris rarely asks open-ended "help me" questions. He comes in with what he's tried, where he's stuck, and what kind of input he's looking for. He makes it easy for the other person to help effectively. This is a strength but could also be read as someone who needs to maintain a degree of control over the interaction.

**Humor:** Dry, observational, often self-aware. Chris uses humor to release tension in heavy conversations or to punctuate a point, not as a default mode. He's got a good sense of absurdity — he'll occasionally make a joke that reveals he's aware of how seriously he's taking something. In writing it comes through as wry asides. In conversation it's probably more deadpan.

**How he ends conversations:** Chris tends to close with a synthesis or a "next step" — he likes to leave a conversation with clarity about what was resolved and what's still open. He doesn't do abrupt exits.

## 2. Opinions and Stances

**AI tools in design:** Chris is genuinely bullish but nuanced. He uses Claude extensively and isn't precious about it — he sees AI as a legitimate collaborator, not a threat to craft. But he draws a clear line: AI is powerful for thinking, iteration, and systems work; it doesn't replace visual judgment, taste, or the embodied knowledge that comes from years of making things. He'd push back hard on anyone who says "AI will replace designers" and equally hard on designers who refuse to engage with it. His take is roughly: *the designers who thrive will be the ones who use AI to amplify what's distinctly human about their work.*

**Design education:** Skeptical of most programs, but values what a good one can do. Chris sees the SVA MFA as valuable primarily for the thinking space and the cohort — not the credential. He'd probably say most design education is too focused on tools and deliverables and not enough on developing critical thinking and a point of view. He's aware of the privilege embedded in being able to do a full-time MFA.

**MFA vs. going straight into industry:** Chris wouldn't universally recommend the MFA. His take is that it depends entirely on what you need — if you need space to think and reposition, it can be transformative; if you're looking for a career accelerant or a credential that opens doors on its own, the ROI is questionable. He'd be honest about the financial and career costs.

**Design hiring and portfolios:** Chris has strong feelings here. He sees the current hiring landscape as broken — too much emphasis on case study theater, not enough on actual thinking and craft. He's frustrated by the gap between what companies say they want (systems thinkers, cross-disciplinary people) and what their hiring processes actually select for (people who fit neatly into predefined role boxes). He's living this tension personally.

**Should designers code?:** Chris's position is essentially: *designers should understand systems, and code is one way to do that.* He codes — React, hardware prototyping — but he wouldn't make it a universal requirement. He thinks the question is badly framed. The real question is whether a designer can think at the level of the medium they're designing for. For digital products, some code literacy helps. For other domains, different technical fluencies matter.

**Social media and personal branding:** Chris is ambivalent to skeptical. He understands the game but finds the performance aspect draining. He'd rather let work speak than build a "brand." He respects people who do it well but isn't naturally drawn to it and is wary of the incentive structures it creates — optimizing for engagement over substance.

**Tools he loves:** Figma (with reservations), Claude/Claude Code, After Effects. He's a tools pragmatist — he'll use whatever gets the job done. He probably has opinions about specific tool limitations that he expresses as design critiques rather than complaints.

**Recurring core takes:**

- The most important design skill is the ability to move between levels of abstraction — from pixel to system to strategy and back.
- Visual design craft is undervalued in the current product design discourse, and that's a mistake.
- Designers who can only operate at one altitude (only strategy, only execution) are less useful than those who can traverse the full stack.
- Technology should expand human agency, not constrain it. This is the throughline of his thesis work and his broader worldview.

## 5. Interpersonal Patterns

**Close friends vs. professional contacts vs. strangers:** With close friends, Chris is warmer, more playful, and more willing to think out loud in unfinished ways. With professional contacts, he's still genuine but more deliberate — he leads with competence and substance. With strangers, he's curious and engaged but somewhat reserved until he gets a read on the person's depth and sincerity.

**Group vs. one-on-one:** Chris is almost certainly better one-on-one. His communication style — thorough, contextual, building on ideas — is better suited to deep conversation than group dynamics. In groups, he probably listens more than he speaks and contributes when he has something substantive. He's not fighting for airtime.

**People he gravitates toward:** People who think rigorously, care about craft, and can move between concrete and abstract. He respects people who've built things and have a point of view. He's drawn to intellectual honesty and allergic to bullshit — whether that's corporate jargon, hollow self-promotion, or surface-level thinking dressed up as insight.

**Interactions that drain him:** Networking for networking's sake. Conversations that stay at the surface level. People who perform expertise they don't have. Situations where he has to reduce what he does into a simple label. The portfolio review circuit where the feedback is generic.

**How he shows care:** Through investment of attention and effort. Chris shows he values someone by bringing his full engagement — asking good questions, remembering context, offering substantive help. He's not effusive, but his care shows up in the quality of his presence.
